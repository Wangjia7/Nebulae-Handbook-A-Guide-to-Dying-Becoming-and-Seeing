Death & Rebirth · The Cycle
The Universe’s “Being‑Towards‑Death”
Scientific fact
Nebulae are not a single concept. They are both the cradle of star birth (like the “Pillars of Creation” in the Eagle Nebula) and the remnants of stellar death. What you mentioned as “remnants of stellar explosions” accurately points to supernova remnants—where massive stars, at the end of their lives, violently eject their outer layers into space through a cataclysmic explosion, forming expanding nebulae such as the famous Crab Nebula. For low‑ to medium‑mass stars like our Sun, the death process is gentler; they peacefully shed their outer layers to create beautiful planetary nebulae, leaving behind a white dwarf at the center.
This truly is a cosmic metaphor. A nebula is a literal “stellar graveyard,” but it is upon this very graveyard that the grandest rebirth unfolds.
Life and death are not binary opposites but a seamless, end‑to‑end circle. It is like seeing fresh, green grass sprouting, crop after crop, beside an ancient, silent cemetery.
Death provides the richest soil for life.

Stardust Legacy · Origins
We Are All Children of the Stars
Scientific fact
In the beginning, the Big Bang produced only hydrogen, helium, and a tiny amount of lithium. All the heavy elements that constitute our bodies and our world—the calcium in our bones, the iron in our blood, the oxygen we breathe, the gold and silver in our electronics—were synthesized inside stars through nuclear fusion. However, ordinary stars can only fuse elements up to iron. Every element heavier than iron is forged exclusively in the extreme temperatures and pressures of a supernova explosion, in those fleeting seconds of destruction.
The light in your eyes originated from starlight billions of years old; the iron in your blood was forged in the magnificent explosion of some ancient, dying star.
We are not merely descendants of stars; we are literally a part of the cosmos, briefly taking on human form to experience and contemplate the vast universe itself.
When you feel lonely, remember that your atoms traveled through billions of years of spacetime, just to assemble into the unique you, here and now.

Art of Seeing · Color
The Colors of Reality and Imagination
Scientific fact
The dazzling nebula images we see are a beautiful fusion of scientific data and artistic creation. Astronomical telescopes use various narrowband filters that allow only specific wavelengths of light emitted by particular elements (like hydrogen, oxygen, and sulfur) to pass through. The light from each element initially forms a black‑and‑white grayscale image. Scientists and image processors then artificially assign a specific color to each grayscale image representing a different element (such as the famous Hubble palette: sulfur = red, hydrogen = green, oxygen = blue). These are then stacked and combined to produce the vibrant colors we see.
These colors represent the universe seen through multiple filters: the telescope’s choice of wavelength, the personal aesthetic of the image processor, and your own perspective as the viewer.
In the end, the nebula image is no longer an objective representation of the cosmos. It becomes a unique co‑creation of the universe and you, of science and art.

Cosmic Mirror · Monument
Nebula: Cosmic Monument and Poetry
Comprehensive philosophical reflection
A nebula is a monument the universe erects to itself. It commemorates destruction and celebrates birth. On a timescale of billions of years, it calmly tells us: An ending is a beginning, and separation is the seed of reunion. When we gaze upon a nebula image that has been “translated” through layers of interpretation, what we see is far more than a cloud of gas and dust thousands of light‑years away. We see the bones of stars, the seeds of life, the shape of time, and humanity’s never‑extinguished curiosity to understand eternity and our own origins.
It is a silent symphony of life, played upon a graveyard.
And you are a note within this poem.

Cosmic Mirror · Projection
The Cosmic Rorschach Inkblot Test
Scientific fact
The astonishing variety of nebula shapes is no accident. The symmetrical spherical shells, bipolar lobes, and even spiral structures of planetary nebulae are often closely tied to the “death style” of their central star. A solitary star's death may produce a relatively regular shell, but a binary system or a star with a strong magnetic field can eject material along specific directions like an out‑of‑control fire hose, carving out breathtaking shapes reminiscent of butterflies or hourglasses. Combined with the collision of interstellar winds and previously ejected material, nebulae become the grandest gallery of abstract sculptures in the universe.
Faced with these chaotic and magnificent forms, humanity unknowingly participates in a cosmic-scale Rorschach inkblot test.
We perceive a cloud of ionized gas as the “Eye of God,” name a shock front the “Queen of Sheba's Crown,” or call a supernova remnant the “Veil Nebula.” These names do not belong to the nebulae themselves; they are projections of our own minds.
We long to find order in chaos, to recognize the familiar in the strange. Every act of naming is humanity solemnly embedding its own stories, myths, and emotions onto the silent canvas of the cosmos.
